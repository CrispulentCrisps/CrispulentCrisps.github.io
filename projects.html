Dragon's Harbour

This game was made in 3rd year at university. For this I had written all 10 tracks of music, implemented the audio into the engine and acted as an additional programmer for the team.

I had learned how to better work in a team in a more cohesive manner and sort out any issues in the team early on. This helped the team work together better and make a solid puzzle platformer.

The game revolves around a child who refuses to sleep until their father reads them a bed time story. The child does not like how the tory goes and constantly changes what the story is as the game is played. The game is a puzzle paltformer where the player can record their actions and have a clone repeat it, using the clones to press switches and levers to get to the right side of the screen.

We Three!

We Three is a puzzle platformer that follow 3 characters, Fisher; Springer; and Stone. They have been tasked to find interesting items in different dimensions for a musuem display, alongside a Crisp £25 reward. Using their different shapes, sizes and abilities they help each other traverse the landscapes and find such interesting items and get back to jump to the next dimension.

For this project I had learned to be more proactive in learning and researching into audio for games in a modern setting. I had learned the relevance between film and game music, while keeping their differences in mind for use in the game.

World without sound

I made this game in 2nd year at university, experimenting with sound synthesis and vertical layering in game music.

The game relies on music to platform towards collectables in the level, with the music slowly layering in as more is collected. Once all collectables have been gathered the player can find a door to exit from.

I had learned how to make my own audio managers with no middleware. This decision was made since I saw it as unessecary considering the game's complexity (or lackthereof). As a reuslt, I had learned how to take more intiiatve with catering my code towards the needs of the game rather than a general one-size-fits-all solution. This helps keep the codebase minimal and helps for a more fundamnetal understanding of audio APIs and how they operate.
